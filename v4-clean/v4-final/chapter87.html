The First Word

Eigentlich, übrigens

The Final Pair of Modal Particles, and a Synthesis of All Eight. These words shift conversations—hedging thoughts and changing topics gracefully. Together with the six particles you've already learned, they complete the emotional vocabulary of native German speech.

Scroll to master the eight particles↓

You have journeyed through eight particles now. In Chapter 85, you met doch, mal, and ja. In Chapter 86, you encountered halt, eben, and schon. Now you meet the final two: eigentlich and übrigens.

These two particles complete your understanding. Together, the eight form a coherent system—the emotional foundation of German speech. Mastering these particles is the step that transforms you from someone who knows German words and grammar into someone who thinks and feels in German.

"These final two particles are the hinges upon which German conversation turns. They shift direction, soften intentions, and change subjects gracefully. They are the particles of real, human speech."

◆ ◆ ◆

Let us begin with eigentlich.

Particle #7
eigentlich
Hedging • Shifting • Softened Contradiction
Core Meaning
The particle eigentlich (which also exists as an adverb meaning "actually" or "really") signals a shift or a softened contradiction. It introduces a thought that partially contradicts or complicates what was just said. It is the particle of hedging—of reconsidering, of changing course mid-thought. When you add eigentlich to a sentence, you are subtly suggesting "upon further reflection" or "well, now that I think about it."
Examples in Context
Eigentlich wollte ich nicht kommen.
Actually, I didn't want to come. (A softened revelation of your actual intention.)
Was machst du eigentlich?
What do you actually do? (Genuine curiosity; asking for clarification.)
Das ist eigentlich nicht so schlecht.
Actually, that's not so bad. (A softened positive reassessment.)
Ich hätte eigentlich lieber Wasser.
I would actually prefer water. (A polite correction or preference shift.)
Two Faces of Eigentlich
As a particle, eigentlich softens contradictions. As an adverb, it means "really" or "actually," providing straightforward information. The context determines which use is operative. In "Eigentlich wollte ich nicht kommen," eigentlich is a particle (softening a contradiction). In "Das ist eigentlich wahr," eigentlich is more adverbial (meaning "really" or "in fact").
Emotional Tone
Thoughtful. Reconsidering. Soft. Not harsh or aggressive. The tone of someone who is giving it more thought and coming to a slightly different conclusion.
Chinese Equivalent
其实 (qíshí) — actually; 说到底 (shuōdàodǐ) — when you get down to it
Particle #8
übrigens
Topic Pivot • "By the Way" • Conversation Shift
Core Meaning
Übrigens (literally "by the way" or "incidentally") signals a shift in topic. It announces that you are about to say something that is not directly connected to the previous line of conversation, but that you want to mention now because you've just remembered it or because it occurred to you. It is the particle of conversational pivots—the word that lets you change subjects gracefully without jarring your listener.
Examples in Context
Das Projekt geht gut voran. Übrigens, hast du gehört, dass Anna umzieht?
The project is progressing well. By the way, did you hear that Anna is moving?
Übrigens, ich wollte dir sagen...
By the way, I wanted to tell you... (A tangential thought you've just remembered.)
Das ist eine gute Idee. Übrigens, wusstest du schon, dass wir nächste Woche eine Konferenz haben?
That's a good idea. By the way, did you know we have a conference next week?
Social Function
Übrigens allows you to introduce new information without making it seem like a non sequitur. It signals "I'm changing the subject now, and here's why: I just remembered this." It softens the topic shift and makes the conversation flow more naturally.
Emotional Tone
Casual. Friendly. The tone of someone who has just remembered something and wants to share it. Sometimes conspiratorial or gossipy, depending on what follows.
Chinese Equivalent
顺便说一句 (shùnbiàn shuō yī jù) — by the way; 对了 (duì le) — oh right; 话说回来 (huàshuō huíláilai) — by the way; speaking of which

The Particles as a System

At this point, you may be asking: "Why these eight particles? Why not more? Why these specific ones?" The answer lies in how German speakers actually think about emotion and emphasis. These eight particles form a complete system because they address the most common emotional needs in conversation:

The Emphasis Layer
Doch and ja emphasize and insist. They make statements louder, more obvious, more emphatic. They are the voice when you need to be heard or when something is self-evident.
The Softening Layer
Mal softens and polishes. It takes a hard request and makes it a suggestion. It is the particle of politeness and casualness.
The Acceptance Layer
Halt and eben accept what cannot be changed. They are the voice of resignation, of philosophical calm, of acknowledgment that some things simply are.
The Encouragement Layer
Schon encourages and reassures. It is the voice of optimism, of confidence that things will work out.
The Shifting Layer
Eigentlich and übrigens shift and pivot. They change direction, soften contradictions, and introduce new topics. They are the voice of reconsideration and topic change.
From Textbook German to Real German
A textbook sentence might be: "Der Mann ist gross." (The man is tall.) It is grammatically correct. It communicates the information. But it is flat. It has no emotional texture. No native German speaker would say this in isolation. Instead, they would say something like: "Der Mann ist ja wirklich gross!" (ja — making it obvious), or "Der Mann ist eigentlich ziemlich gross." (eigentlich — softening a possible contradiction), or "Der Mann? Der ist halt gross." (halt — accepting it as fact). The particle chosen reveals how the speaker feels about the statement. This is what native German sounds like.
Your Next Steps
You have now learned the eight core modal particles. But learning them and using them are different things. To move from recognition to production, you should: (1) Notice particles in German speech and writing. When you encounter them, pause and feel the emotional texture they add. (2) Try to use one particle per day in your own speech or writing, even if you are not sure if it is correct. Mistakes with particles are much less serious than mistakes with grammar. (3) Listen to German music, films, and podcasts, and notice how native speakers use particles naturally. (4) Understand that mastering particles is a gradual process. You will not suddenly "get" them. Instead, over time, they will become second nature.
