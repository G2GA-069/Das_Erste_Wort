Chapter Eighty-Seven

Eigentlich, übrigens

Modal Particles: Conversation Shifters
↓

We reach the final set of modal particles—particles that shift conversations in real time. Where previous particles adjusted tone and attitude, these particles actively steer dialogue. They change the direction of thought, redirect attention, or circle back to main points after digression. They are the particles of conversation itself: the tools that speakers use to navigate discourse, to bring discussions back on track, to hedge their meaning, or to redirect focus.

Eigentlich, übrigens, jedenfalls, allerdings, nämlich—these are the particles of dialogue. They move conversation forward, backward, and sideways.

Understanding these particles means understanding how German speakers actually think and communicate in real conversation. These are the particles you hear in native speech, shaping the flow of discussion, softening statements, and guiding listeners through the mental landscape of the speaker's thoughts. Master these, and you will understand not just what Germans say, but how they organize and express their thinking.

The Architecture of Thought

German conversation uses particles to build structures of thought. Eigentlich hedges and redirects. Übrigens introduces asides. Jedenfalls returns to the main point. Allerdings concedes before continuing. Nämlich explains reasons. Together, these particles form a kind of conversational architecture—they show how a German speaker's mind moves through an idea, where they emphasize, where they qualify, where they digress and return. Learning these particles is learning to think in German.

Eigentlich means "actually" or "originally," but its particle function is far more subtle and powerful.

eigentlich
actually, originally, in fact (hedging, redirecting, reframing)

Eigentlich as a modal particle is the tool of redirection and hedge. It says: what I just said (or what we've been discussing) is not quite what I actually mean. Let me reconsider, reframe, or add nuance. "Das ist eigentlich nicht so einfach." (Actually, it's not that simple.) The speaker begins by seeming to agree or understand, then uses eigentlich to redirect the conversation toward a more accurate or nuanced position.

Eigentlich can hedge and soften direct statements. "Ich bin eigentlich nicht damit einverstanden." (Actually, I don't agree with that.) It sounds less harsh than "Ich bin nicht damit einverstanden"—the eigentlich acknowledges the other person's point while creating space for disagreement. It says: I hear you, but actually, let me reconsider aloud.

In questions, eigentlich can signal confusion or a request for clarification. "Was willst du eigentlich?" (What exactly do you want?) or "Warum sind Sie eigentlich hier?" (Why exactly are you here?) The eigentlich adds a sense of "I thought I understood, but actually, let me ask again." It's not accusatory, but it signals that the speaker's understanding was incomplete.

Eigentlich can also introduce a realization or afterthought. "Eigentlich wollte ich dir etwas anderes sagen." (Actually, I wanted to tell you something else.) The speaker was about to say one thing, then uses eigentlich to redirect toward what they actually meant to say. It transforms the conversation, redirecting its course.

This particle is essential to how Germans actually speak. It's the word of reconsideration, of intellectual honesty, of saying "wait, let me think about this more carefully." It marks German speakers as thoughtful and willing to revise their positions in light of new understanding.

Das ist eigentlich nicht so einfach.
Actually, it's not that simple. (reframing the discussion)
Ich bin eigentlich nicht damit einverstanden.
Actually, I don't agree with that. (softened disagreement)
Was willst du eigentlich?
What exactly do you want? (seeking clarification)
Eigentlich wollte ich dir etwas anderes sagen.
Actually, I wanted to tell you something else. (redirecting the conversation)
Das hättest du eigentlich wissen sollen.
You actually should have known that. (mild reproach)
Eigentlich bin ich ganz anderer Meinung.
Actually, I'm of quite a different opinion. (hedged disagreement)

Übrigens is the particle of tangent and aside—the conversational detour.

übrigens
by the way, incidentally, speaking of which (topic shift, aside)

Übrigens marks a shift in topic. It says: what I'm about to tell you is not the main point, but it's worth mentioning anyway. "Übrigens, hast du schon von seinem neuen Job gehört?" (By the way, have you heard about his new job?) The speaker is making a conversational detour—they remember something tangential, and übrigens signals this shift to the listener.

Übrigens often appears at the start of sentences, but it can also appear mid-sentence to signal that something is being added as an aside. "Das ist wahr, und übrigens, ich habe das schon früher gesagt." (That's true, and by the way, I said that before.) The speaker is adding a tangential point, acknowledging its tangential nature with übrigens.

The particle is crucial for natural dialogue. It shows the listener that the speaker knows they're changing topics, that it's a conscious shift, not a derailment. "Übrigens" says: I know this is off-topic, but I want to mention it anyway. It's honest about conversational tangents, acknowledging them rather than pretending they're part of the main thread.

Übrigens can also be used to introduce information that you think is worth remembering. "Übrigens, der Film kommt nächste Woche raus." (By the way, the film comes out next week.) The speaker is saying: you might find this worth knowing, even though it's not the main subject we're discussing.

Übrigens, hast du schon von seinem neuen Job gehört?
By the way, have you heard about his new job? (tangential shift)
Das ist wahr, und übrigens, ich habe das schon früher gesagt.
That's true, and by the way, I said that before. (adding an aside)
Übrigens, vergessen wir nicht, morgen anzurufen.
By the way, let's not forget to call tomorrow. (introducing aside)
Ich bin einverstanden, übrigens, hat er das auch gesagt?
I agree, and by the way, did he say that too? (tangential addition)
Übrigens, der Film kommt nächste Woche raus.
By the way, the film comes out next week. (sharing tangential information)

Jedenfalls is the particle of return—it brings conversation back to the main point.

jedenfalls
in any case, anyway, at least (returning to main point)

Jedenfalls serves as the conversational anchor. After a digression, a qualification, or an aside, jedenfalls signals: let's return to the main point. "Das ist kompliziert, aber jedenfalls sollten wir etwas tun." (It's complicated, but in any case, we should do something.) The speaker acknowledges complexity, then uses jedenfalls to move beyond it toward action or decision.

Jedenfalls can also mark a fallback position or a minimum claim. "Ich weiß nicht, was genau passiert ist, aber jedenfalls sollte das nicht noch einmal geschehen." (I don't know exactly what happened, but in any case, that should not happen again.) The speaker is saying: I'm uncertain about details, but I'm certain about this minimum claim.

In longer discussions, jedenfalls functions as punctuation—it marks the return from tangent to main thread. It says to the listener: we've explored the complications and the asides, now let's get back to business. It's the particle of pragmatism that says "enough nuance, let's decide."

Das ist kompliziert, aber jedenfalls sollten wir etwas tun.
It's complicated, but in any case, we should do something. (returning to main point)
Ich weiß nicht, was genau passiert ist, aber jedenfalls...
I don't know exactly what happened, but in any case... (marking minimum claim)
Es gibt viele Gründe dafür und dagegen, jedenfalls müssen wir entscheiden.
There are many reasons for and against it, anyway, we have to decide. (moving to decision)
Das ist jedenfalls besser als vorher.
At least that's better than before. (fallback position)

Allerdings is the particle of concession—it acknowledges before continuing.

allerdings
however, admittedly, I must say, though (concession)

Allerdings concedes validity before the speaker adds their own point. "Das stimmt allerdings." (That's admittedly true.) The speaker acknowledges that the other person is right, but the allerdings signals that something follows—a qualification, a "but," a different perspective. It's intellectually honest: I recognize the validity of what you're saying, and here's my addition or caveat.

Allerdings can also appear at the beginning of a response, signaling: "I hear your point, and I must admit it has merit, but..." "Allerdings, das hätte ich auch sagen können." (Admittedly, I could have said that too.) The speaker is acknowledging the other person's wit or insight before adding their own.

In longer arguments, allerdings functions as honest acknowledgment. Rather than dismissing opposing points, the German speaker uses allerdings to recognize them while continuing with their own argument. "Du hast recht, allerdings..." (You're right, although...) This is the particle of intellectual fairness—it says: I will not pretend your point is invalid, but I have something to add.

Das stimmt allerdings.
That's admittedly true. (conceding validity)
Allerdings, das hätte ich auch sagen können.
Admittedly, I could have said that too. (acknowledging other's point)
Du hast recht, allerdings...
You're right, although... (conceding before continuing)
Das ist allerdings schwieriger als es aussieht.
That is, admittedly, more difficult than it looks. (acknowledging difficulty)
Das ist allerdings ein guter Punkt.
That is, I must say, a good point. (recognizing merit)

Nämlich is the particle of explanation—it introduces the reason why.

nämlich
namely, you see, because (explanation, reason)

Nämlich introduces explanation and reason. "Ich kann nicht kommen, ich bin nämlich krank." (I can't come, you see, I'm sick.) The nämlich says: here comes the reason, the explanation for what I just said. It's often used in the middle of a sentence to provide the reasoning behind a statement, as if the speaker is explaining to themselves as much as to the listener.

Nämlich can appear as a sentence starter: "Nämlich, das Problem ist..." (You see, the problem is...) or "Das geht nicht, nämlich weil..." (That won't work, namely because...) The particle signals that explanation is coming, that the listener should pay attention to the reason being offered.

In conversation, nämlich often appears when a speaker realizes they haven't fully explained themselves. "Das ist wichtig, nämlich weil die Zukunft davon abhängt." (That's important, you see, because the future depends on it.) The speaker adds nämlich to clarify their reasoning, to help the listener understand not just the claim, but why the speaker believes it.

Ich kann nicht kommen, ich bin nämlich krank.
I can't come, you see, I'm sick. (providing reason)
Das geht nicht, nämlich weil zu viele Menschen betroffen sind.
That won't work, namely because too many people are affected. (explaining why)
Das ist wichtig, nämlich weil die Zukunft davon abhängt.
That's important, you see, because the future depends on it. (clarifying reason)
Nämlich, das Problem ist...was wollen wir eigentlich erreichen?
You see, the problem is...what exactly do we want to achieve? (introducing central question)
Das sehe ich anders, nämlich...
I see that differently, namely... (offering alternative explanation)

The Parallel in Chinese

Chinese also has conversation-directing particles. The word 其实 (qíshí, "actually") serves functions similar to eigentlich. The expression 顺便说一下 (shùnbiàn shuōyīxià, "by the way") parallels übrigens. Where German uses modal particles to guide conversation, Chinese uses similar tools—particles and expressions that show listeners how the speaker is organizing their thoughts. Both languages encode the architecture of thought in their particles. Learning these particles in any language is learning to see how that culture thinks, plans, and navigates discourse.
