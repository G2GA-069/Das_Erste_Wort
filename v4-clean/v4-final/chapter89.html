Idioms II

Treasures of the German Language — Cultural Expressions That Reveal the German Soul. These idioms are windows into how Germans think, feel, and navigate their world. Each carries centuries of history and cultural wisdom.

Scroll to discover ancient expressions↓

Language is not merely a tool for communication—it is a treasury of cultural memory. Every idiom holds a story, a moment in history where a people observed something about life and crystallized it into an unforgettable phrase. German idioms are particularly rich because they are so concrete, so tied to the physical world and human experience. To master them is to think like a German.

"Die Sprache trägt die Seele einer Nation in ihren Worten." — Language carries the soul of a nation in its words.

Da steppt der Bär
Literally: "The bear is dancing there"
Meaning: It's a great party! Things are lively and exciting. People are having a wonderful time.
Historical Origin
This idiom dates back to medieval times when bear-baiting was a popular form of public entertainment throughout German-speaking lands. A bear would be brought to the town square, often on market days or during festivals, and spectators would gather to watch the animal perform tricks or engage in combat. The phrase "da steppt der Bär" refers to the animated, almost frenzied energy of a bear dancing or moving under duress—but over time, it became a metaphor for the festive, energetic atmosphere of the crowd itself. When people were having such a good time that the atmosphere was almost wild, they would say "da steppt der Bär!"
Example Sentences
"Auf dem Oktoberfest da steppt der Bär! Überall Musik, Tanz und Lachen."
"Auf der Hochzeit meines Bruders da steppt der Bär—es ist fantastisch!"
Chinese Equivalent
热闹非凡 (rènào fēifán) — extraordinarily lively and bustling
Die Kirche im Dorf lassen
Literally: "Leave the church in the village"
Meaning: Don't exaggerate. Keep things in perspective. Be reasonable. Tone it down.
Historical Origin
This idiom emerged in rural Germany during the medieval period when villages were the fundamental unit of social organization. The church was always the central, most important building in any village—it was the anchor of community life, the gathering place for all important events. The phrase arose from the observation that people sometimes became carried away with grandiose plans or made excessive claims. When someone was proposing something too ambitious or trying to make a small village matter seem more important than it was, the townspeople would say, "Lass die Kirche im Dorf!"—essentially, "Keep the church in the village," meaning "Keep proportions realistic" and "Don't blow this out of proportion." The church represents what is real, fundamental, and true; leaving it in the village means maintaining a sense of reality and proportion.
Example Sentences
"Es war nur ein kleiner Fehler, lass die Kirche im Dorf!"
"Mach keine große Sache daraus—lass die Kirche im Dorf lassen."
Chinese Equivalent
别大题小作 (biè dà tí xiǎo zuò) — don't make a big deal out of small things
Öl ins Feuer gießen
Literally: "Pour oil into the fire"
Meaning: Make things worse. Inflame a situation. Add fuel to the conflict. Escalate tensions.
Historical Origin
This idiom is based on obvious physical reality—pouring oil on fire makes flames larger and more dangerous. It likely emerged from practical experience with cooking fires and heating fires in households. The image is so powerful and universally understood that it has been used in German for centuries as a metaphor for making conflicts worse. Oil doesn't extinguish fire; it accelerates and amplifies it. So when someone is adding provocative statements, escalating arguments, or introducing inflammatory information, they are figuratively "pouring oil on the fire."
Example Sentences
"Deine Kommentare gießen nur Öl ins Feuer! Das macht alles schlimmer."
"Statt zu helfen, gießt du Öl ins Feuer der Debatte."
Chinese Equivalent
火上浇油 (huǒ shàng jiāo yóu) — literally identical! "Pour oil on fire." This demonstrates how across cultures, people use the same physical metaphor to express the concept of making things worse.
Über den Tisch ziehen
Literally: "Pull (someone) over the table"
Meaning: Cheat or deceive someone. Take advantage of them. Swindle them. Pull a fast one.
Historical Origin
This idiom likely originates from medieval merchant culture or card games where deals and transactions took place across a table. The physical act of "pulling someone over the table" would represent a sudden, aggressive move to gain advantage—perhaps pulling away goods, money, or documents. In the context of card games or negotiations, being "pulled over the table" suggests being outmaneuvered or cheated. The table becomes a metaphor for the space where fair dealings occur; to pull someone over it suggests violating that fairness and engaging in deception.
Example Sentences
"Der Verkäufer hat mich über den Tisch gezogen—das Auto war völlig kaputt."
"Pass auf, dass dich niemand über den Tisch zieht!"
Chinese Equivalent
被骗 (bèi piàn) — to be cheated; 上当受骗 (shàng dàng shòu piàn) — to be fooled and cheated
Auf dem Holzweg sein
Literally: "Be on the timber path" or "Be on the wood path"
Meaning: Be on the wrong track. Be mistaken. Bark up the wrong tree. Go in the wrong direction.
Historical Origin
This idiom comes from the practical realities of forest work in German-speaking regions. Timber paths (Holzwege) were temporary paths cut through forests to extract wood. Unlike regular roads that led to villages and settlements, these paths were purposeful but temporary—they led only to the logging sites and then ended, leading nowhere. A traveler who mistakenly took a Holzweg thinking it was a proper road would find themselves deep in the forest with no destination in sight. This became a powerful metaphor: if you are "auf dem Holzweg," you are heading in the wrong direction, following a false path that will not lead you anywhere productive.
Example Sentences
"Deine Theorie ist völlig auf dem Holzweg. Das funktioniert nicht so."
"Wenn du denkst, dass das die Lösung ist, bist du auf dem Holzweg."
Chinese Equivalent
走错了路 (zǒu cuò le lù) — to take the wrong road; 南辕北辙 (nán yuán běi zhé) — heading south while wanting to go north (going in the opposite direction)
Nicht alle Tassen im Schrank haben
Literally: "Not have all cups in the cupboard"
Meaning: Be crazy or mentally unbalanced. Not be quite right in the head. Be eccentric or unstable.
Historical Origin
This idiom comes from a domestic household metaphor. A proper household, a well-ordered home, has all its cups and dishware intact and arranged properly in the cupboard. If cups are missing—if the set is incomplete—something is amiss, something is wrong with the order of the house. This becomes a metaphor for mental completeness: if someone is missing cups from their mental cupboard, they are not quite mentally whole or stable. The image suggests that having a complete set of cups represents being complete and sane, whereas missing cups represent missing mental faculties or instability.
Example Sentences
"Er hat nicht alle Tassen im Schrank—seine Ideen sind verrückt."
"Manche Menschen haben einfach nicht alle Tassen im Schrank."
Chinese Equivalent
神经不正常 (shénJīng búzhèngcháng) — nerves not normal; 疯了 (fēng le) — is crazy
Kein Blatt vor den Mund nehmen
Literally: "Not take a leaf before the mouth"
Meaning: Speak bluntly. Be honest and direct. Not hold back. Speak your mind without filtering.
Historical Origin
This idiom's origins are somewhat mysterious but likely involve social courtesy and shame. In various cultures, a leaf or hand held before the mouth can symbolize modesty, shame, or the filtering of one's words. To take no leaf before the mouth means to offer no filter, no barrier between one's thoughts and one's speech. It suggests complete honesty and the absence of social pretense. Some scholars suggest it may relate to ancient practices of covering the mouth when ashamed or when speaking something shameful, so the reverse—taking nothing before the mouth—means speaking without shame or hesitation.
Example Sentences
"Sie nimmt kein Blatt vor den Mund—sie sagt einfach, was sie denkt."
"Ich nehme kein Blatt vor den Mund: dein Plan wird nicht funktionieren."
Chinese Equivalent
直言不讳 (zhíyán búhuì) — speak directly without hesitation; 开诚布公 (kāi chéng bù gōng) — be frank and open
Ins Fettnäpfchen treten
Literally: "Step into the grease dish"
Meaning: Commit a social faux pas. Make an embarrassing mistake. Offend someone by accident.
Historical Origin
This charming idiom comes from household kitchens, particularly from the practice of keeping rendered animal fat (Fett) in small dishes or containers. In old kitchens, these small dishes of grease sat on the floor or in accessible places. Someone walking through a kitchen might accidentally step into one of these dishes, getting greasy material on their shoes and making a mess—a social embarrassment if you were a guest or if the kitchen was being kept tidy. The act of stepping into the grease dish became a perfect metaphor for making an embarrassing social mistake: just as stepping in grease is something you do accidentally that causes awkwardness and mess, a social faux pas is an unintended action that creates awkwardness in a social situation.
Example Sentences
"Oh nein, ich bin ins Fettnäpfchen getreten! Ich wusste nicht, dass sein Vater gerade verstorben ist."
"Pass auf, dass du nicht ins Fettnäpfchen trittst—das ist ein sensibles Thema für sie."
Chinese Equivalent
犯错误 (fàn cuòwù) — to make a mistake; 失礼 (shī lǐ) — to be impolite or disrespectful; 触犯禁忌 (chù fàn jìnjì) — to violate a taboo
Beyond the Words: Understanding German Thinking
These idioms collectively reveal something profound about the German mindset. A German speaker thinks about problems as "paths" that can be wrong (auf dem Holzweg sein). They think about society as needing "proportion" (Kirche im Dorf lassen). They understand that actions have consequences that escalate situations (Öl ins Feuer). They value honesty and directness (kein Blatt vor den Mund) while also being acutely aware of social proprieties (ins Fettnäpfchen treten). They see human nature with a touch of humor and pragmatism (nicht alle Tassen im Schrank). This is not random. German culture, shaped by its geography (surrounded by other cultures and nations), its history (centuries of principalities and city-states before unification), and its philosophical tradition (from Kant to Hegel to Wittgenstein), has produced a language that reflects these characteristics. When you master these idioms, you are not just learning expressions—you are learning how to think in German.
Recognition vs. Production: Building Confidence
Your journey with these idioms will have two phases. First comes recognition: when you hear or read "da steppt der Bär," you immediately understand it means a lively party atmosphere. This phase is easier and will come first. Second comes production: being able to use these expressions naturally in your own speech and writing. This is harder and takes longer. The good news is that recognition typically precedes production by only a few months if you are actively studying and engaging with German media. To accelerate your movement into the production phase, try the following: (1) Write example sentences of your own, (2) Listen for these idioms in German films and podcasts, (3) Notice the exact contexts where native speakers use them, (4) Try to use one idiom per week in conversation if possible, (5) Understand that even native speakers don't overuse idioms—they appear naturally in conversation but not constantly. The goal is to expand your emotional and contextual understanding so that when the moment comes to use one, it feels natural rather than forced.

These eight idioms represent the core vocabulary of German expressivity. They appear constantly in speech, literature, and film. Understanding them means understanding not just the German language, but the German way of seeing the world—concrete, practical, deeply connected to history and tradition. Notice how many of them originate in rural life, household experience, and practical occupations. This reveals a fundamental German characteristic: the tendency to ground abstract concepts in concrete, observable reality.

The Culture of German Idioms
German idioms are remarkable because they reveal a people deeply observant of their physical world. Unlike languages that might use abstract metaphors or poetic imagery, German idioms tend to draw from the ordinary, the practical, and the observable. A bear dancing. A church staying in its village. Oil burning on fire. Cups in a cupboard. Grease on a floor. These are things anyone who lived in the German-speaking lands would have directly observed and understood. This practical grounding made these expressions memorable across generations and cultures. They were not invented by poets in isolation; they were born from shared human experience translated into memorable images. This is why they have survived for centuries and continue to be used naturally in modern German, even in the age of smartphones and digital life.
How to Use These Idioms Authentically
As a learner of German, your goal should be not just to know these idioms but to use them naturally. This requires understanding three things: (1) The core meaning must be crystal clear, (2) You must understand the social context where the idiom is appropriate, (3) You should know variations or related expressions. For example, "Öl ins Feuer gießen" is most appropriate when discussing conflicts, heated debates, or situations getting worse. You would not use it to describe someone making a minor mistake. The intensity of the idiom must match the situation. Similarly, "kein Blatt vor den Mund nehmen" is appropriate when someone is being unusually direct or honest, sometimes to the point of being tactless. It carries a sense of admiration or sometimes gentle criticism, depending on context. Using idioms correctly means reading the situation and matching the idiom's emotional intensity to the moment.
Variations and Related Expressions
German has numerous variations on some of these idioms. For instance, instead of saying someone "hat nicht alle Tassen im Schrank," you might hear similar expressions like "nicht alle Latten im Zaun haben" (not all slats in the fence) or "nicht ganz bei Trost sein" (not quite with one's wits). These variations carry slightly different nuances but express similar ideas. In the same way, "Öl ins Feuer gießen" might be expressed as "Benzin ins Feuer gießen" (pouring gasoline on fire) for extra intensity, or "noch ein Wort und ich gießen Öl ins Feuer" (one more word and I will pour oil on fire). These variations demonstrate how idioms are living language—they evolve, they have related expressions, and native speakers often modify them slightly to fit particular situations while maintaining core meaning.
