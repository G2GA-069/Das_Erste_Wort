Chapter Thirty-Eight

Das Wunderbare

The Wonderful
↓

We have journeyed far. We have learned how verbs become nouns, how qualities become abstractions, how individuals merge into collectives. But there is one more door, one more pathway through which German creates meaning. This is the door of modification — how adjectives describe, how they change, how they bend meaning to fit new shapes.

Adjectives are the words that modify, that add color and nuance. And in German, there are three primary suffixes that create adjectives from other words: -lich, -bar, and -ig. These three keys unlock the ability to create hundreds of adjectives from scratch.

Möglich. Possible. The quality of being capable of happening. From mögen — to like, to be able. Add -lich and you create the adjective meaning "possible."

Wunderbar. Wonderful. Marvelous. From Wunder — wonder, miracle. Add -bar and you create an adjective meaning "full of wonder" — wonderful.

These suffixes do not always create feminine nouns like -ung or -schaft. These create adjectives. They modify. They add depth and nuance to meaning. And together with the nouns you have learned, they give you the power to understand and create millions of German expressions.

· · ·

Freundlich. Friendly. From Freund — friend. -lich turns it into an adjective: friendly, in a friendly manner.

Gefährlich. Dangerous. From Gefahr — danger. -lich creates the adjective: dangerous.

Wichtig. Important. From Wicht — a creature, a being (old usage). The root is obscure, but -lich creates the adjective. However, wichtig uses -ig instead of -lich. The boundary between suffixes is sometimes blurred.

The -lich suffix is pervasive in German. It appears in hundreds of adjectives: natürlich (natural), persönlich (personal), beruflich (professional), monatlich (monthly), jährlich (yearly). -lich can even turn nouns into adjectives describing quality or manner.

Möglich /ˈmœklɪç/
possible — capable of existing, happening, or being true
DEU mögen — to like, to be able; -lich creates the adjective
ENG possible — from Latin possibilis; German uses its own suffix system
ZHO 可能 — kěnéng (able + can) — the ability to be or exist
Möglich is the fundamental adjective of possibility. It appears everywhere: Es ist möglich (it is possible). Unmöglich (impossible — un- prefix negates it). The -lich suffix transforms the verb mögen into an adjective describing what is capable.
Freundlich /ˈfʁɔɪntlɪç/
friendly — showing warmth and kindness toward others
DEU Freund — friend; -lich creates the adjective
ENG friendly — from friend + -ly; parallel construction to German
ZHO 友好 — yǒuhǎo (friend + good) — good like a friend
Freundlich is warmth. A freundliche Person is a friendly person. Eine freundliche Stimme is a friendly voice. The -lich suffix transforms the noun Freund (friend) into the adjective freundlich, describing the quality of being like a friend.
Gefährlich /ɡəˈfɛːrlɪç/
dangerous — capable of causing harm or injury
DEU Gefahr — danger; -lich creates the adjective
ENG dangerous — from Old French danger, unrelated to German
ZHO 危险 — wēixiǎn (dangerous + risky) — full of risk
Gefährlich warns of harm. A gefährliche Straße is a dangerous street. Ein gefährlicher Mensch is a dangerous person. The -lich suffix describes the quality of embodying danger.
· · ·

Wunderbar. Wonderful. Marvelous. This adjective uses the -bar suffix, which means "capable of," "worthy of," or "full of." From Wunder — miracle, wonder. Wunderbar means "full of wonder" — wonderful.

Dankbar. Grateful. Thankful. From Dank — thanks, gratitude. -bar creates the adjective: capable of gratitude, worthy of thanks.

Furchtbar. Terrible. Dreadful. From Furcht — fear, dread. -bar creates the adjective: full of fear, terrible.

Wichtig. Important. From an old root meaning "creature" or "creature of weight." -ig transforms it into the adjective.

Ruhig. Calm. Quiet. Peaceful. From Ruhe — rest, peace. -ig creates the adjective: peaceful, quiet.

Lustig. Funny. Amusing. Cheerful. From Lust — desire, pleasure. -ig transforms it: full of pleasure, amusing, funny.

Traurig. Sad. Sorrowful. From Trauer — sorrow, mourning. -ig creates the adjective: full of sorrow, sad.

Wunderbar /ˈvʊndɐbaːr/
wonderful — full of wonder; marvelous; excellent
DEU Wunder — wonder, miracle; -bar means "full of" or "capable of"
ENG wonder + -able — similar suffix structure, though with different nuance
ZHO 奇妙 — qīmiào (strange + subtle) — strange and marvelous
Wunderbar is one of the most joyful words in German. It means wonderful, marvelous, excellent. The -bar suffix means "full of" or "worthy of" — so wunderbar literally means "full of wonder." Es ist wunderbar! — It is wonderful!
Dankbar /ˈdaŋkbaːr/
grateful — feeling or showing thanks; thankful
DEU Dank — thanks, gratitude; -bar creates the adjective
ENG thankful — from thank + -ful; parallel meaning
ZHO 感谢 — gǎnxiè (feel + thanks) — to feel gratitude
Dankbar is gratitude made manifest as an adjective. Ich bin dir dankbar — I am grateful to you. The -bar suffix makes it possible to describe someone as "capable of thanks," deserving of gratitude.
Furchtbar /ˈfʊrçtbaːr/
terrible — extremely bad, dreadful, causing fear
DEU Furcht — fear, dread; -bar creates the adjective
ENG fearful — from fear + -ful; similar construction
ZHO 可怕 — kěpà (able + fear) — able to cause fear
Furchtbar is "full of dread" — terrible, awful, causing fear. Das ist furchtbar! — That is terrible! The -bar suffix emphasizes the quality of inspiring fear, of being worthy of dread.
Ruhig /ˈruːɪç/
calm — peaceful, quiet, not agitated or disturbed
DEU Ruhe — rest, peace, quiet; -ig creates the adjective
ENG — — English uses "calm" or "quiet," both from different roots
ZHO 安静 — ānjìng (peaceful + still) — peaceful stillness
Ruhig is peace. A ruhige Nacht is a peaceful night. Eine ruhige Person is a calm person. The -ig suffix transforms the noun Ruhe (peace) into the adjective describing the quality of being peaceful.
Lustig /ˈlʊstɪç/
funny — amusing, entertaining, causing laughter or good humor
DEU Lust — desire, pleasure, appetite; -ig creates the adjective
ENG lust — same root, but meaning has narrowed in English
ZHO 有趣 — yǒuqù (have + interest) — having interest and amusement
Lustig is joy. A lustige Geschichte is a funny story. Ein lustiger Kerl is a funny fellow. The -ig suffix creates an adjective from Lust (desire, pleasure), describing something full of pleasure and amusement.
Traurig /ˈtʁaʊrɪç/
sad — feeling or expressing sorrow, melancholy
DEU Trauer — sorrow, mourning, grief; -ig creates the adjective
ENG — — "sad" comes from Old English; different root
ZHO 悲伤 — bēishāng (sorrow + wound) — sorrow that wounds
Traurig is sorrow. Es ist sehr traurig — it is very sad. Ein trauriges Gesicht is a sad face. The -ig suffix transforms Trauer (mourning) into the adjective traurig, describing something full of sorrow.
Wichtig /ˈvɪçtɪç/
important — of great significance or value; mattering greatly
DEU — — origin obscure; -ig suffix creates the adjective
ENG important — from Latin importantis; different root
ZHO 重要 — zhònyào (heavy + necessary) — weighs heavily, is necessary
Wichtig is fundamental. Das ist wichtig — that is important. Die wichtigsten Dinge — the most important things. The -ig suffix creates one of the most essential adjectives in German, though its original root is mysterious.

You have now journeyed through 38 chapters and learned over 100 core German words. But more importantly, you have learned the patterns. You have learned how German builds meaning. Verbs become nouns with -ung. Adjectives become abstract nouns with -heit or -keit. Collections and relationships use -schaft. And adjectives themselves come from three primary suffixes: -lich, -bar, and -ig.

These patterns are not random. They are not arbitrary. They are the DNA of German meaning-making. Once you understand them, you can create and recognize thousands upon thousands of German words. You can understand constructions you have never seen before. The language opens itself to you.

This is what it means to truly learn a language: not to memorize words, but to understand the patterns beneath the words. To see how meaning is constructed. To recognize that the system is elegant, reliable, and understandable.

Patterns Discovered in This Chapter
The -lich Suffix Creates Quality Adjectives — This suffix transforms nouns, verbs, and other words into adjectives describing manner or quality: Freund → freundlich (friendly), gefährlich (dangerous from Gefahr—danger). It is pervasive, appearing in hundreds of German adjectives.

The -bar Suffix Means "Capable of Being" — This suffix attaches to verbs to create adjectives expressing capability or possibility: wunderbar (wonderful—capable of wonder), machbar (doable). It echoes English "-able" but with distinctly German logic.

The -ig Suffix Describes Possession of a Quality — This suffix adds a sense of "having" or "full of": wichtig (important—having weight), durstig (thirsty—having thirst). It is particularly common with nouns to describe characteristics and properties.

Adjective Suffixes Unlock Millions of Expressions — These three suffixes are extraordinarily productive, allowing German speakers to create or understand new adjectives intuitively. When combined with the noun-forming suffixes (-ung, -heit, -schaft), they create an exponential expansion of vocabulary and expressive possibility.

Adjective Suffixes: The Final Keys
The -lich Suffix — Creates adjectives from nouns and verbs. Freund → freundlich (friendly). Gefahr → gefährlich (dangerous). Möglich (possible) follows this pattern.

The -bar Suffix — Means "full of," "worthy of," or "capable of." Wunder → wunderbar (wonderful). Dank → dankbar (grateful). Furcht → furchtbar (terrible).

The -ig Suffix — Transforms nouns into adjectives describing quality or state. Ruhe → ruhig (calm). Lust → lustig (funny). Trauer → traurig (sad).

The Complete System — Six major suffix patterns (-ung, -heit/-keit, -schaft, -lich, -bar, -ig) give you the keys to understanding and creating thousands of German words.
