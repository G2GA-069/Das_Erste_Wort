Chapter Eighty-Four

Die falschen Freunde II

False Friends: Part Two
↓

We have learned eight false friends. Now we continue deeper into the maze. The walls are marked with misleading signs. Each word points in a wrong direction. Each one is a test of whether you have truly learned the difference between appearance and reality. These ten false friends are often encountered in everyday German, and each one is a trap for the unwary traveler.

The second set of false friends is sometimes more insidious than the first. They hide in plain sight. They live in the words you use every day. Their danger lies not in their rarity, but in their frequency.

· · ·

The first false friend in this set relates to factories and textiles.

Fabrik looks like it might relate to fables or fabric, but it refers to neither. The confusion comes from similar-looking words with completely different origins.

Fabrik
factory, manufacturing plant, works, mill, industrial facility
Etymology: From Latin "fabrica" (workshop, craft, manufacture). The German word kept the Latin meaning of industrial production. English "fabric" also comes from the same Latin root but evolved to mean woven material, while "factory" took another path entirely from "factorium."
English "fabric" is material used for clothing and textiles. German "Fabrik" comes from Latin "fabrica" (workshop, craft), meaning factory or manufacturing plant. The English word "fabric" also traces to the same Latin root but evolved to mean specifically woven material. German kept the industrial meaning. "In der Fabrik arbeiten" = "to work in a factory." English has "factory" (also from Latin), while "fabric" went a different direction toward textiles. This false friend is dangerous because English speakers who study German expect "Fabrik" to relate to "fabric," leading them to imagine textile mills when the word could refer to any factory—cars, machinery, chemicals, electronics. The divergence is complete: one language narrowed the meaning to textiles, the other kept it broad for manufacturing.
Wrong: "I work with Fabrik." (sounds like you work with cloth)
Right: "Ich arbeite in der Fabrik." (I work in a factory)
Memory trick: "Fabrik" = factory. "Fabric" = cloth. Keep them separate. A Fabrik makes things, Fabric IS a thing.

The second false friend is about speed, though not quite in the way you might expect.

Fast looks like the English word "fast," but German uses it differently. The meaning is inverted from what speed normally suggests.

fast
almost, nearly, practically, virtually, just about
Etymology: From Old High German "fast" or "fasto," originally meaning "firmly" or "fixed." The meaning evolved to mean "almost" through the sense of being "almost fixed" or nearly certain. The English word "fast" (quick) comes from a different root entirely and diverged completely.
English "fast" means quick or moving at high speed. German "fast" means almost, nearly, practically. "Das ist fast fertig" = "That's almost finished." "Fast alle" = "Almost all." The confusion is immediate and profound when an English speaker hears "Das Auto fährt fast" and thinks the car is moving quickly, when in fact it means "The car is almost [something]." This is perhaps one of the cruelest false friendships, because both sound and spelling are nearly identical, yet the meanings could not be more different. One is about velocity, the other about approximation. German has "schnell" for quick or fast in the speed sense. The irony is that German "fast" actually means something that is "almost" true or "nearly" the case, which is the opposite of "fixed" or "certain," showing how word meanings can invert over centuries.
Wrong: "The car drives fast." → "Das Auto fährt fast." (means "The car almost drives")
Right: "The car drives quickly." → "Das Auto fährt schnell." (means "The car drives quickly")
Memory trick: "Fast" = almost. "Fast" (English) = speedy. One means nearly-complete, the other means rapid. Opposite concepts, identical spelling!

The third false friend is about logical connection and sequence.

Also looks like English "also," but it means something entirely different. The similarity is deceptive.

also
so, therefore, thus, consequently, well then, so then, and so
Etymology: From Old High German "alsô," from "al" (all) + "sô" (so). Originally meant "in such a manner" or "so therefore." English "also" evolved from a different root (Germanic "al-" + "so-") to mean "additionally" or "in addition," while German kept the causal sense.
English "also" means "in addition" or "furthermore." German "also" means "so," "therefore," or "consequently." "Also, wir müssen gehen" = "So, we must go" or "Therefore, we must go." "Also das ist ein Problem" = "Well then, that's a problem." The word in German signals logical consequence or introduces a conclusion, not something additional. This false friend causes German learners to read English into sentences where only logical connection is meant. "Also" in German is almost conversational—it introduces what comes next as a logical consequence of what came before. The meanings are nearly opposite: English "also" adds information, German "also" draws conclusions.
Wrong: "I like ice cream and also pizza." → "Ich mag Eis und also Pizza." (doesn't sound right)
Right: "I like ice cream. Also, pizza is good." → "Ich mag Eis. Also, Pizza ist gut." (logical connection)
Memory trick: German "also" = so/therefore. English "also" = additionally/too. One is about logical sequence, the other about addition.

The fourth false friend concerns written marks and musical notation.

Note in German does not mean a brief written message. The word refers to something more official and permanent.

Note
grade, mark, score (in school), musical note
Etymology: From Latin "nota" (mark, sign, note). Both English and German inherited the word, but English evolved it toward informal written messages and observations, while German kept it for marks/grades and musical notes primarily.
English "note" is a brief written message or observation. German "Note" primarily means a grade or mark in school, or a musical note. "Eine gute Note bekommen" = "To get a good grade." "Die Noten spielen" = "To play the notes." English has "Note" for musical notation, but it's also used for grades. German uses "Notiz" (from the same Latin root) for a brief written note or observation, while "Note" is reserved for grades and musical notation. This false friendship catches German learners who expect "Note" to mean a written reminder or message, when it actually refers to something much more official and evaluative. The grade connotation is particularly important in German culture, where "Noten" are central to academic assessment.
Wrong: "I wrote a Note about the meeting." (sounds like you wrote a grade)
Right: "Ich schrieb eine Notiz über das Treffen." (I wrote a note about the meeting)
Memory trick: German "Note" = grade/mark. English "note" = brief message. For a written message in German, use "Notiz."

The fifth false friend is about categories and art, but not quite in the way you might think.

Art does not mean artwork in German. The word refers to something much more fundamental and categorical.

Art
kind, type, sort, way, manner, species
Etymology: From Latin "ars" (art, skill, craft). Both English and German inherited the Latin word. English evolved it toward creative work and aesthetics, while German retained the categorical sense of "kind" or "type."
English "art" primarily means creative visual work or aesthetic expression. German "Art" means kind, type, sort, or manner. "Eine Art von Obst" = "A kind of fruit." "Auf diese Art" = "In this way" or "In this manner." German has "Kunst" for art in the aesthetic sense, not "Art." The false friendship here is subtle but dangerous for learners: "Art" in German is nearly invisible in meaning—it's a basic categorical word like "kind" or "type." The irony is that English borrowed the Latin word and narrowed it dramatically to aesthetic creation, while German kept it broad and ordinary. A German speaker saying "Das ist eine andere Art" is not talking about creative work at all, but about a different kind or type of something.
Wrong: "I saw beautiful Art in the museum." (in German, sounds odd)
Right: "Ich sah schöne Kunstwerke im Museum." (I saw beautiful works of art)
Memory trick: German "Art" = kind/type. German "Kunst" = art (creative). English "art" = creative work. Keep "Kunst" for aesthetics.

The sixth false friend is about numbers, and it is perhaps the most dangerous for anyone dealing with large quantities.

Billion does not mean one thousand million in German. This false friend has confused mathematicians, economists, and business professionals for generations.

Billion
trillion (in American English), one million million (10^12), one thousand billion
Etymology: From Latin "bi-" (two) + "million" (from "mille," thousand). English "billion" evolved to mean 10^9, while German (and many European languages) kept the older meaning of 10^12. This difference stems from long-scale vs. short-scale number naming conventions that diverged in the 20th century.
This is perhaps the most dangerous false friend of all, because it deals with money, commerce, and official statistics. English "billion" means one thousand million (10^9 or 1,000,000,000). German "Billion" means one million million (10^12 or 1,000,000,000,000). The difference is a factor of one thousand—which is enormous in financial and statistical contexts. "Eine Billion Euro" in German is one trillion euros in English—one thousand times larger than an English "billion." This false friendship has caused real confusion in international business, finance, and journalism. Economists must be careful when translating statistics between German and English. The German "Milliarde" (from "Milliarde" = thousand million) means what English calls a "billion." This is a case where historical naming conventions created a mathematical trap. The long-scale system (used in Germany and much of Europe) versus the short-scale system (used in English-speaking countries) means these words are not synonymous despite looking identical.
Wrong: "Eine Billion Euro" = "$1 billion USD" (actually $1 trillion USD)
Right: "Eine Milliarde Euro" = "$1 billion USD" (actually ~$1.1 billion USD)
Memory trick: German "Billion" = TRILLION (10^12). German "Milliarde" = billion (10^9). A thousand times difference! Always check context.

The seventh false friend relates to pensions and income.

Rente is not about paying rent for housing. The word refers to a much more pleasant financial concept.

Rente
pension, annuity, retirement income, regular income from investments
Etymology: From Old French "rente" (income from capital). German kept the original meaning. English "rent" evolved to mean specifically payment for use of property, narrowing the meaning over time.
German "Rente" comes from Old French "rente" (income from capital), while English "rent" evolved from the same root but narrowed to mean specifically payment for use of property. German kept the broader financial meaning. "In Rente gehen" = "to go into retirement" or "to start receiving a pension." "Die gesetzliche Rente" = "state pension." "Eine private Rente" = "a private pension." This word will confuse English speakers instantly, as "Rente" and "rent" look and sound similar but mean completely different things. In German, "Miete" is the word for rent paid for housing. The false friendship is dangerous for anyone dealing with German retirement planning or financial documents. "Rente" is central to German social insurance terminology, making it a word you'll encounter frequently in official contexts.
Wrong: "I pay Rente for my apartment." (implies you're receiving retirement income for your apartment!)
Right: "Ich zahle Miete für meine Wohnung." (I pay rent for my apartment)
Memory trick: German "Rente" = pension/retirement. German "Miete" = rent. English "rent" = payment for housing. Keep them separate.

The eighth false friend relates to donating and spending money.

Spenden looks like it might relate to "spend," but the word means something more generous and charitable.

spenden
to donate, to give (charitably), to contribute, to bestow
Etymology: From Latin "spendere" (to allot, to pay). The German word kept the sense of giving or allocating, while English "spend" evolved to mean using money generally on purchases.
English "spend" means to use money on purchases, generally for one's own use. German "spenden" means to donate or give charitably. "Ich spende Geld für eine wohltätige Organisation" = "I donate money to a charitable organization." German "ausgeben" is the word for "spend" in the English sense—using money on purchases for yourself. The false friendship here is subtle: both words relate to money and giving something away, but "spenden" specifically means charitable giving, while "spend" is neutral and generally selfish. A German speaker saying "Ich spende viel Geld" is describing charitable giving, not personal spending. This is a particularly interesting false friend because it reveals a cultural difference: German has a specific word for charitable giving, while English uses the same word for any use of money.
Wrong: "I spenden money on new clothes." (implies you're donating them)
Right: "Ich gebe Geld für neue Kleider aus." (I spend money on new clothes)
Memory trick: German "spenden" = donate. German "ausgeben" = spend. English "spend" = use money. Different concepts, different words in German.

The ninth false friend relates to academic achievement and career advancement.

Promotion does not mean advancement in your job. In German academia, this word refers to something quite different and equally prestigious.

Promotion
doctoral degree, PhD, doctoral dissertation, the conferring of a doctorate
Etymology: From Latin "promovere" (to move forward, to advance). English "promotion" evolved to mean career advancement or marketing promotion, while German "Promotion" kept the sense of formal academic advancement to doctorate level.
English "promotion" means advancement in one's job or marketing of a product. German "Promotion" means completing a PhD and receiving a doctoral degree. "Seine Promotion abschließen" = "To complete one's PhD" or "To defend one's dissertation." "Eine Promotion anstreben" = "To pursue a doctorate." The word is commonly used in German academic contexts to refer to the entire process of doctoral research and degree conferral. German has "Beförderung" for job advancement. This false friend is particularly tricky in academic contexts where English speakers expect marketing or career advancement when Germans are discussing doctoral education. The cultural difference is significant: German academia places great emphasis on the "Promotion," which is the formal path to "Herr Doktor" or "Frau Doktor" status.
Wrong: "I got a Promotion at work." (in German, sounds like you got a PhD!)
Right: "Ich wurde befördert." (I was promoted [at work])
Memory trick: German "Promotion" = PhD/doctorate. German "Beförderung" = job promotion. English "promotion" = job advancement. Different paths to success!

The tenth and final false friend in this set is about magical objects and architectural features.

Wand is not a magical stick. The word refers to something much more ordinary and structural.

Wand
wall (of a room or building), interior wall
Etymology: From Old High German "wand" or "want," possibly related to "wenden" (to turn). The German word refers to a wall or barrier, while English "wand" comes from Old Norse "vöndr" (straight stick) and evolved to mean a thin stick, especially magical ones.
English "wand" is a thin stick, typically associated with magic (magician's wand, wizard's wand). German "Wand" is a wall of a building or room. "Die Wand streichen" = "To paint the wall." "An der Wand hängt ein Bild" = "A picture hangs on the wall." Harry Potter fans learning German encounter this immediately—a "Zauberstab" is a wizard's wand (literally "magic stick"), not a "Wand." The false friendship is amusing but easy to catch in context. German has "Zauberstab" (literally "magic stick") for the magical stick, while "Wand" is purely architectural. The divergence is absolute: one is thin and magical, the other is large and structural.
Wrong: "The wizard waved his Wand." (in German, he waved a wall!)
Right: "Der Zauberer schwenkte seinen Zauberstab." (The wizard waved his magic stick)
Memory trick: German "Wand" = wall (of a building). German "Zauberstab" = magic wand. English "wand" = magic stick. Very different objects!

Recognizing Patterns in False Friends: The second set of false friends reveals deeper patterns. Some words narrow in meaning (English "art" to aesthetics only), others broaden (German "Art" remains open to "kind" or "type"). Some reverse in connotation (German "fast" = nearly, English "fast" = speedy). Some divide into two separate words (German "spenden" = donate, "ausgeben" = spend). The most dangerous false friends are those you encounter frequently—like "fast," "also," "Note," and "Billion"—because their danger lies not in their rarity but in their regular misuse. Understanding these patterns helps you develop an instinct for spotting false friends you haven't yet encountered.
