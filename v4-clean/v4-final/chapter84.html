False Friends II

More Deceptive Words, Plus the Opposite: True Friends. Not every similar word is a false friend. Some English-German word pairs DO mean the same thing across both languages — these are true friends (cognates), and they're the linguistic gift that makes this whole journey possible.

Scroll to discover betrayal and trust↓

Now that you understand how false friends work, let's continue with more deceptive words. These will deepen your awareness of the dangers lurking in linguistic similarity. And then — here's the reward — we'll examine the opposite phenomenon: true friends, words that really do mean the same thing in both languages. These true friends are not just safe havens; they're proof that the linguistic inheritance between English and German is fundamentally strong, even if some cousins have wandered astray.

"For every false friend, there are ten true friends. The language is fundamentally honest. It's just that the betrayals are memorable."

brav
Meaning: Well-behaved (NOT brave/courageous)
Why It's a False Friend
English "brave" means courageous or fearless. German brav means well-behaved, obedient, or dutiful. "Ein braves Kind" means "a well-behaved child," not "a courageous child." For courage, Germans use mutig.
The Etymology
Both words likely come from Old Germanic or Latin roots meaning "worthy" or "excellent," but diverged dramatically. English evolved "brave" toward physical courage, while German kept brav more focused on moral compliance and good behavior.
eventuell
Meaning: Possibly/maybe (NOT eventually/in the end)
Why It's a False Friend
"Eventually" in English means at some point in the future or finally. German eventuell means possibly or maybe (in the present or near future). "Eventuell komme ich später" = "Maybe I'll come later," not "I will eventually come later."
The Etymology
Both come from Latin "eventualis." English narrowed it to "at some future point." German kept it broader to mean "possibly" or "contingently." A subtle but critical difference in meaning.
Konzept
Meaning: Draft/rough plan (NOT a finished concept)
Why It's a False Friend
English "concept" is an idea or abstract thought. German Konzept is specifically a draft, a preliminary plan, or a rough outline. "Ich habe ein Konzept für das Projekt" = "I have a draft plan for the project," suggesting something preliminary, not final.
The Etymology
Both from Latin "conceptum." But German semantically shifted it toward the written or documented draft/plan, while English kept it as abstract idea.
Mappe
Meaning: Folder/portfolio (NOT a map)
Why It's a False Friend
Looks like "map" but means folder or portfolio in German. "Deine Mappe ist voll Papieren" = "Your folder/portfolio is full of papers." For a map, Germans use Karte or Landkarte.
The Etymology
German Mappe likely comes from Latin "mappa" (cloth), and evolved toward a document folder or portfolio. English "map" comes from the same root but specialized to geographical maps.
also
Meaning: So/therefore (NOT also/as well)
Why It's a False Friend
This is a critical false friend. English "also" means "as well" or "in addition." German also means "so" or "therefore." "Also, was machen wir?" = "So, what do we do?" NOT "Also, we do what?" For "also" (as well), Germans use auch.
The Etymology
Both come from Latin "allso" or similar roots. But the words diverged. English kept it as an additive conjunction. German shifted it to a logical conjunction meaning "so" or "therefore."
fast
Meaning: Almost (NOT fast/quick)
Why It's a False Friend
English "fast" means quick or rapid. German fast means almost or nearly. "Das ist fast fertig" = "That's almost finished," not "That's finished quickly." For quick, Germans use schnell.
The Etymology
These words don't even share roots. German fast probably comes from Old High German "fasta" meaning "hardship" or "strain," evolving toward "almost" (as in "almost didn't make it"). English "fast" comes from Old Germanic meaning "fixed" or "firm" (fast = firmly fixed, therefore quick). Pure coincidence that they sound identical.
See
Meaning: Lake (der See) OR sea (die See) — gender determines meaning
Why It's a False Friend (and complicated)
See is fascinating: the masculine form der See means a lake. The feminine form die See means the sea. Same spelling, different genders, opposite meanings. This is a unique linguistic quirk where gender is the decoder.
The Etymology
German has two separate words that happen to be homophones: See (lake, from Old Germanic), and See (sea, from Old High German). Different origins, same pronunciation, different genders. The gender tells you which one is meant: der See = lake (masc.), die See = sea (fem.).

Now for the antidote to false friends: TRUE FRIENDS. These are cognates — words that really do mean the same thing in English and German. They're proof that the two languages are fundamentally siblings, even if some cousins have become unreliable.

Finger TRUE FRIEND ✓
Meaning: Finger — exactly what it means in English
The Trust
Finger means finger in German, same as in English. A true friend that you can always rely on. From Proto-Germanic roots that have remained stable for thousands of years.
Butter TRUE FRIEND ✓
Meaning: Butter — the dairy product, same as in English
The Trust
Butter is identical in meaning across both languages. From Latin "butyrum," both English and German kept the same meaning and form for this essential food.
Wanderlust TRUE FRIEND ✓
Meaning: A strong desire to travel and explore (English borrowed this directly from German!)
The Trust
Wanderlust is a compound: "Wander" (to hike/roam) + "Lust" (desire). English borrowed this word directly from German in the 19th century. When English speakers use "wanderlust," they're speaking German.
Kindergarten TRUE FRIEND ✓
Meaning: A school for young children (English borrowed this German word too!)
The Trust
German compound: "Kinder" (children) + "Garten" (garden). Literally, a "children's garden" — a place where young minds grow like plants. English borrowed this term entirely, recognizing its perfection.
Rucksack TRUE FRIEND ✓
Meaning: A backpack (English borrowed this directly from German)
The Trust
German compound: "Ruck" (back) + "Sack" (sack/bag). A bag you carry on your back. English adopted this term in the 19th century. True friend from the start.
Angst TRUE FRIEND ✓
Meaning: Anxiety/dread/existential fear (English borrowed this from German psychology)
The Trust
Angst in English means a sense of anxiety or dread, especially existential anxiety. This word was borrowed from German philosophy and psychology (think Kierkegaard, Heidegger) in the 20th century. It's a true friend and a gift to English.
◆ ◆ ◆

The lesson? The relationship between English and German is fundamentally honest. For every false friend, there are dozens of true friends waiting to be discovered. Finger, Butter, Wanderlust, Kindergarten, Rucksack, Angst — these words prove it. English didn't just borrow words; it borrowed concepts. It borrowed German ways of seeing the world. And for every word that betrays you, ten more will confirm your intuition. Trust the language. Just check yourself with the dangerous ones.

Patterns Discovered: False Friends II & True Friends

This chapter introduces a critical insight: Linguistic divergence is not random. False friends typically follow patterns of semantic narrowing, semantic shift, or compound repurposing. True friends demonstrate that the inheritance between English and German is fundamentally strong.

Key insight: English borrowed certain German words wholesale (Wanderlust, Kindergarten, Rucksack, Angst), recognizing their conceptual perfection. This is not weakness in German; it's recognition of semantic precision.
