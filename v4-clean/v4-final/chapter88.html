The First Word

Redewendungen I

Common German Idioms and Their Stories. Idioms are the final frontier of language learning. They cannot be decomposed or translated literally. They must be learned as wholes. Yet their origins—often rooted in everyday German life—reveal how Germans think about the world.

Scroll to decode the spirit of German language↓

You have now mastered the patterns, the grammar, the emotional particles. You understand how German words are built and how sentences are constructed. You have traveled through time from Proto-Indo-European to modern Berlin. You have learned the decoder ring that makes 80% of German vocabulary visible to English speakers.

But there is one final layer. There are expressions that cannot be decoded. They cannot be translated word-for-word. They must be learned as complete units, and their meanings must be internalized as wholes. These are Redewendungen—idioms. And they reveal something profound: the way a culture sees the world. German idioms are especially revealing because they tend to be concrete, practical, and rooted in observable reality.

"Idioms are the poetry of everyday speech. They reveal how a people think, what they value, and how they see the world. They are not optional—they are essential to native-level fluency."

Da steppt der Bär
Literally: "The bear is dancing there"
Meaning: It's a great party; things are lively and fun. The atmosphere is energetic and celebratory.
Historical Origin
This idiom dates back to medieval times, when traveling performers and circuses included trained bears as entertainment. A bear "dancing" (actually performing trained movements) would draw crowds and create excitement. The image of a dancing bear became synonymous with lively entertainment and celebration. When a party is so fun that it feels like a bear is dancing, the energy is obvious and contagious.
Example Sentences
"Das ist ja eine tolle Party! Da steppt der Bär!"
"Im Tanzclub da steppt wirklich der Bär."
Chinese Equivalent
欢呼雀跃 (huāny call joyfully); 热闹非凡 (rènao fēi fán) — very lively
Die Kirche im Dorf lassen
Literally: "Leave the church in the village"
Meaning: Don't exaggerate; keep things in proportion; don't make a mountain out of a molehill.
Historical Origin
In rural German communities, the church was the center of village life—literally and figuratively. A village without its church was unthinkable. The phrase suggests: keep the important things (the church) in their proper place (the village), rather than inflating minor issues out of proportion. The church remains where it belongs—grounded, centered, not elevated beyond measure.
Example Sentences
"Das Problem ist nicht so groß. Lass die Kirche im Dorf!"
"Du dramatisierst zu viel. Lass die Kirche doch im Dorf."
Chinese Equivalent
保持理智 (bǎochí lǐzhì) — keep your senses; 不要小题大做 (búyào xiǎo tí dà zuò) — don't make a big deal of small things
Öl ins Feuer gießen
Literally: "Pour oil on the fire"
Meaning: Make things worse; escalate conflict; add fuel to the fire in a situation that is already tense.
Historical Origin
This idiom is pure practical logic. Fire needs fuel to grow. Oil is highly flammable. Pouring oil on a fire makes it worse—much worse. The image is immediate, visceral, and universally understood. Any action that intensifies conflict, makes a tense situation worse, or adds to existing problems is pouring oil on fire.
Example Sentences
"Mit deinen Worten gießt du nur Öl ins Feuer."
"Jetzt Öl ins Feuer gießen? Das ist eine schlechte Idee."
Chinese Equivalent
火上浇油 (huǒ shang jiāo yóu) — add oil to fire; 雪上加霜 (xuě shang jiāshūang) — frost on snow; 越来越坏 (yuè lái yuè huài) — make things worse
Auf dem Holzweg sein
Literally: "Be on the timber path"
Meaning: Be on the wrong track; be mistaken; be heading in the wrong direction.
Historical Origin
In German forests, there were often temporary logging paths—"Holzwege"—used to extract timber. These paths were different from main roads. A traveler following a timber path thinking it was the main route would eventually realize they were lost. The path led nowhere permanent, nowhere meaningful. Being "on the timber path" became a metaphor for being misled, mistaken, or heading in the wrong direction.
Example Sentences
"Deine Theorie ist interessant, aber ich denke, du bist auf dem Holzweg."
"Wenn du das so machst, bist du auf dem Holzweg."
Chinese Equivalent
走错路了 (zǒu cuò lù le) — taking the wrong path; 大错特错 (dà cuò tè cuò) — completely wrong
Schwein haben
Literally: "To have pig"
Meaning: To be lucky; to have good fortune; to get lucky.
Historical Origin
The origin of this idiom reaches back to medieval times and old German customs. In some games and contests, the loser would receive a consolation prize—often a pig. Over centuries, the association shifted. Having a pig became associated with winning by luck—receiving something unexpected and valuable. Pigs were valuable animals, so "having a pig" meant having something good befall you by chance. Modern usage simply means: you got lucky.
Example Sentences
"Du hast ja Schwein gehabt! Der Bus kam gerade an."
"Manchmal muss man Schwein haben im Leben."
Chinese Equivalent
走运 (zǒu yùn) — lucky; 碰巧 (pèng qiǎo) — by chance; 幸运 (xìngyùn) — fortunate
Das ist mir Wurst
Literally: "That is sausage to me"
Meaning: I don't care; it doesn't matter to me; I'm indifferent.
Historical Origin
German culture has long revolved around sausage—Wurst—as a staple food. The phrase is irreverent and modern, appearing to originate in the early 20th century. The idea is playful: saying something "is sausage to me" is a way of saying it's completely unimportant, throwaway, or deserving of indifference. The sausage is mundane, ordinary, and hardly worth attention. Thus: "I don't care."
Example Sentences
"Was die Leute über mich sagen? Das ist mir Wurst."
"Das Fußballspiel? Ist mir völlig Wurst."
Chinese Equivalent
我不在乎 (wǒ búzàihū) — I don't care; 我不关心 (wǒ búguānxin) — I'm indifferent
Alles hat ein Ende, nur die Wurst hat zwei
Literally: "Everything has an end, only the sausage has two"
Meaning: All good things must come to an end. Everything has a beginning and an end, except a sausage—which is circular and has two ends.
Historical Origin
This is a playful, somewhat philosophical saying that emerged in German-speaking regions. The humor lies in the twist: sausages are often tied or cased at both ends, so they technically have two ends rather than one. The saying humorously uses this fact to suggest that while all earthly things eventually end, sausages are the exception—they have (at least) two ends. It's a darkly humorous reminder of mortality with a culinary twist.
Example Sentences
"Die schönsten Dinge gehen vorüber. Alles hat ein Ende, nur die Wurst hat zwei."
Chinese Equivalent
天下没有不散的筵席 (tiānxià múyǒu búsàn de yánxí) — All gatherings must come to an end; 好景不长 (hǎo jǐng búcháng) — good times don't last
Klar wie Kloßbrühe
Literally: "Clear as dumpling broth"
Meaning: Crystal clear; perfectly obvious. (Ironic: dumpling broth is typically murky, not clear.)
Historical Origin
This idiom uses irony in a distinctly German way. Dumpling broth (Kloßbrühe) is made by cooking dumplings in broth, and the result is definitely not clear—it's murky, opaque, and cloudy. By saying something is "clear as dumpling broth," you are using irony to suggest the opposite: that something is confusing and unclear. However, over time, the idiom has shifted meanings, and it can now also mean "crystal clear," depending on context and ironic intent. The beauty of the idiom lies in its linguistic ambiguity.
Example Sentences
"Deine Erklärung ist mir so klar wie Kloßbrühe." (Your explanation is unclear to me.)
"Das ist klar wie Kloßbrühe!" (That's obviously unclear!)
Chinese Equivalent
含糊不清 (hánhu búqīng) — unclear; 一团糟 (yī tuán zāo) — a mess
