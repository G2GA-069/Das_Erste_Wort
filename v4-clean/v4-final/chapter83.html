Falsche Freunde I

False Friends I

Words That Sound Like English but Mean Something Completely Different. These are the most deceptive words in German — they look familiar, but they'll trick you every time if you're not paying attention. This chapter teaches you to recognize the divergence.

Scroll to discover linguistic betrayal↓

English and German share thousands of words. But here's the danger: some words look identical or nearly identical, yet mean completely different things. These are the false friends — the words that will betray your confidence if you're not careful. A speaker might say "Das ist ein Gift" and you might think they're giving you a present. You'd be very wrong. Or they might say "Ich bekomme ein Bier" and you might think something is happening to them. Again, you'd misunderstand.

"False friends are the price of linguistic similarity. English and German were siblings once, but centuries of separation have made them deceptive cousins."

The reason these words diverge is simple: the same ancestor word took different semantic journeys in English and German. The word Gift comes from the Proto-Germanic root meaning "something given" (like a poison administered as a gift). English kept that root in the noun "gift" but shifted the meaning to something positive. German kept the word but shifted it to poison. Both are logically traceable to the same origin, but the metaphors diverged over centuries.

Gift
Meaning: Poison (NOT a present)
Why It's a False Friend
English speakers immediately think of "gift" as a present. But in German, Gift means poison. This is one of the most dangerous false friends in the language because it's so easily mistaken.
The Etymology
Both words come from Proto-Germanic *giftiz (something given). In English, the word came to mean "a gift" (something given as a present). In German, the word kept the form but shifted semantically: a Gift was something "given" or "administered" — particularly a poison administered to someone. The metaphor: poison is something "given" to kill. Over time, German fixed this meaning so completely that Gift now means poison exclusively.
When You'll Hear This
Critical context: "Das Pilz hat Gift" (That mushroom is poisonous). "Gift ist tödlich" (Poison is deadly). This is a conversation-critical false friend because misunderstanding it could be dangerous or deeply confusing.
bekommen
Meaning: To receive/get (NOT to become)
Why It's a False Friend
Looks like "become" (the English verb meaning to transform into). In German, bekommen means to receive or to get something. "Ich bekomme ein Bier" means "I'm getting a beer" (receiving one), not "I'm becoming a beer."
The Etymology
The word is related to the verb "bekommen" which combines "be-" (a prefix) + "kommen" (to come). So literally it means "to come to be given" or "to come into possession of." English "become" has a completely different etymology and different evolution. The German word is purely about receiving or obtaining, not transformation.
When You'll Hear This
Constant use: "Ich bekomme einen Brief" (I'm getting a letter). "Sie bekommen ein Paket" (You're receiving a package). "Das Baby bekommt einen Zahn" (The baby is getting a tooth). Critical to understand correctly.
Handy
Meaning: Mobile phone (NOT handy/convenient)
Why It's a False Friend
In English, "handy" means convenient or useful. In German, a Handy is a mobile phone. Germans borrowed the English word "handy" (thinking it meant something small and portable you can hold in your hand) and repurposed it to mean mobile phone. But English speakers will be confused by this usage.
The Story
This is a fascinating case of linguistic borrowing gone wrong. In the 1980s and 90s, as mobile phones became popular in Germany, they needed a word for this new device. The marketing term was often "mobile Telefon" but Germans also latched onto the English word "handy" (thinking it meant "something handy you hold in your hand"). Over time, Handy became the standard German word for a mobile phone. Now, when Germans see the English word "handy," they might not immediately think of "convenient."
When You'll Hear This
Extremely common: "Mein Handy ist kaputt" (My phone is broken). "Hast du dein Handy dabei?" (Do you have your phone with you?). This word will come up constantly in modern German conversation.
Chef
Meaning: Boss/supervisor (NOT a chef/cook)
Why It's a False Friend
English uses "chef" to mean a professional cook. German uses Chef to mean a boss or supervisor of any kind. A restaurant chef in German would be called a Küchenchef (kitchen chief) if you wanted to be specific, but just Chef usually means your boss at work.
The Etymology
Both words come from French "chef" (chief, head). English narrowed it to a professional cook. German kept the broader meaning of "chief" or "boss." In English, the chef is the head of the kitchen. In German, a chef is the head of anything — a department, a company, a team.
When You'll Hear This
Workplace language: "Mein Chef ist sehr streng" (My boss is very strict). "Der Chef möchte dich sehen" (The boss wants to see you). "Sie ist die Chefin des Teams" (She is the female boss of the team — note the -in ending for female forms).
Rat
Meaning: Advice (NOT a rat/rodent)
Why It's a False Friend
English "rat" is a rodent. German Rat means advice. "Gib mir einen Rat" means "Give me some advice," not "Give me a rat." The word for rodent is completely different in German: Ratte.
The Etymology
Rat comes from Old Germanic roots meaning "to advise" or "to counsel." The word evolved to mean both the act of giving advice and the advice itself. You can see this in the place name Rathaus (town hall) — literally "advice house," where the city council (advisors) would meet. English never developed this semantic path; instead "rat" became the name of a rodent.
When You'll Hear This
Common phrases: "Ich gebe dir einen Rat" (I'll give you some advice). "Das ist ein guter Rat" (That's good advice). "Frag deinen Vater um Rat" (Ask your father for advice). Notice: the rodent is Ratte, pronounced slightly differently.
aktuell
Meaning: Current/up-to-date (NOT actual/real)
Why It's a False Friend
English "actual" means real or factual. German aktuell means current, timely, or up-to-date. "Das ist nicht aktuell" means "That's not current" or "That's outdated," not "That's not real."
The Etymology
Both words come from Latin "actualis" meaning "relating to action." But they evolved differently. English "actual" came to mean "real" (related to actions that actually happened). German aktuell came to mean "current" or "of current relevance" (relating to current action and events). The semantic paths diverged over centuries.
When You'll Hear This
News and current events: "Das ist nicht mehr aktuell" (That's no longer current/relevant). "Die aktuelle Situation ist kompliziert" (The current situation is complicated). "Aktuelle Nachrichten" (Current news, today's news).
sympathisch
Meaning: Likeable/nice (NOT sympathetic)
Why It's a False Friend
English "sympathetic" means showing compassion or understanding for someone's suffering. German sympathisch means likeable or pleasant in personality. "Er ist sehr sympathisch" means "He's a very likeable/nice person," not "He's sympathetic to my problems."
The Etymology
Both come from Greek "sympatheia" (suffering together). But German developed the word more in the direction of "pleasant personality" and "good social vibes," while English kept it focused on emotional support and compassion. For "sympathetic" in the emotional support sense, Germans use different words like einfühlsam.
sensibel
Meaning: Sensitive/touchy (NOT sensible/practical)
Why It's a False Friend
English "sensible" means practical or reasonable. German sensibel means sensitive, easily hurt, or touchy. "Sie ist sehr sensibel" means "She's very sensitive (emotionally)," not "She's very practical."
The Etymology
Latin "sensibilis" means "capable of being felt or perceived." English evolved it toward "capable of making sense" (practical reasoning). German kept it closer to the original meaning of "capable of feeling" (emotional sensitivity). They share an ancestor but took completely different paths.
Fabrik
Meaning: Factory (NOT fabric/cloth)
Why It's a False Friend
English "fabric" means cloth or woven material. German Fabrik means a factory or manufacturing plant. Same Latin root, completely different meanings.
The Etymology
Latin "fabrica" originally meant a craftsman's workshop. English narrowed it to "fabric" (the product of weaving). German kept it broader to mean any factory or manufacturing plant (the place where things are made). So a factory is where fabric is made — but in English, the place is called a "mill" or "factory," not "fabric."
Gymnasium
Meaning: Secondary school (NOT a gym/exercise facility)
Why It's a False Friend
In English, a gymnasium is a place where people exercise. In German, a Gymnasium is a type of academic secondary school (high school). A place to exercise is called a Fitnessstudio or Sporthalle.
The Etymology
Greek "gymnasion" originally meant "a place for athletic training and education." In both English and German, the word was borrowed. But English used it narrowly for the athletic training place (shortened to "gym"). German kept the broader Classical meaning: a Gymnasium is an elite academic secondary school where students receive a comprehensive education (classical gymnasion = place of comprehensive education and physical training). The Gymnasium is the highest level of secondary school in Germany, preparing students for university.
◆ ◆ ◆

Understanding false friends is not about memorizing them as isolated facts. It's about recognizing that even though English and German are siblings, centuries of separation have made them deceptive cousins. The same root word can carry entirely different meanings. This is why reading German with English as your first language can be dangerous — you can't always trust your intuition. You must check yourself.

The good news: false friends are rare enough that once you learn them, they become landmarks in your mental map of German. They're memorable precisely because they're surprising. When you learn that Gift means poison, you'll never forget it. When you understand that Handy means mobile phone, it becomes intuitive. The shock of the revelation burns it into memory.

"False friends are not obstacles. They are teachers. Each one teaches you that languages diverge, that meaning shifts, that you cannot assume. They make you a better, more careful reader."

Patterns Discovered: False Friends I

This chapter introduces a critical pattern: Even identical or near-identical words in English and German can diverge dramatically in meaning over centuries of linguistic separation. False friends are not bugs in language learning — they are features. They teach us to read carefully and to understand that linguistic inheritance is not automatic translation.

Key insight: The divergence usually traces back to one of these causes:

  • Semantic narrowing: A broad word becomes narrow (Chef started as "chief," now means "boss")
  • Semantic shift: A word takes on a completely new meaning based on metaphor or context (Gift = "given" → poison as something administered)
  • Borrowed words repurposed: A word borrowed from one language gets redefined in another (Handy = mobile phone in German, but means "convenient" in English)
