Chapter Eighty-Three

Die falschen Freunde I

False Friends: Part One
↓

English and German are cousins, descended from the same Germanic root centuries ago. They share approximately 26,000 cognate words—words that appear similar in both languages. Most cognates are dependable; their similarities point to genuine meaning. But among the cousins, there are imposters. Words that wear the same face but harbor different intentions. Words that look identical yet point in entirely different directions. They are mirrors showing distorted reflections.

We call them falsche Freunde—false friends. These words are particularly dangerous because they offer the appearance of familiarity while concealing radical difference. A false friend that looks German but misleads you away from meaning. A word that tempts you to translate by appearance rather than by understanding.

To master a language is to learn where the false friends hide. It is to understand not similarity, but the places where similarity ends. It is to recognize that two languages can speak the same words yet mean entirely different things.

· · ·

The first false friend we encounter is perhaps the most dangerous because it looks so English.

Bekommen appears to be constructed from familiar Germanic parts. The prefix "be-" is recognizable, the root "kommen" clearly means "to come." One might think: "to become." But this assumption will mislead you into one of the oldest and most dangerous traps in translation.

bekommen
to receive, to get, to obtain, to acquire
Etymology: Old High German "biqueman," from "be-" + "queman" (to come, to reach). Originally meant "to come to, to reach." Over centuries, the meaning narrowed to "to come into possession of."
Bekommen is deceptively English-looking, but it has traveled a different historical path. While English "to come" remained tied to movement, the German "bekommen" evolved to mean receiving or obtaining something. The shift happened over centuries as the word's usage narrowed from "to arrive at" to "to come into possession of." "Ich bekomme einen Brief" = "I receive a letter" or "I get a letter." Never "I become a letter." This false friendship has caused countless mistranslations and embarrassing mistakes. A German speaker saying "Ich bekomme ein Kind" (I'm getting/having a child) will confuse an English speaker who hears it as "I'm becoming a child."
Wrong: "I am becoming a present." ✗
Right: "I'm getting a present." ("Ich bekomme ein Geschenk.") ✓
Memory trick: "bekommen" sounds like "be-come," but it's "be-GET." The word is about receiving, not becoming. Think of "getting" something into your possession.

The second false friend relates to danger in a way that horrified generations of English speakers.

Gift does not refer to a present you wrap for someone's birthday. It refers to something far more sinister—something that has poisoned human history and appears frequently in medieval German literature.

Gift
poison, toxin, venom, lethal substance
Etymology: Old High German "gift," from "geben" (to give). Originally "something given," specifically something administered in a dose. Medieval usage narrowed this to mean specifically poison—something "given" to someone to kill them.
This false friend is particularly dangerous in its implications. English "gift" comes from Old Norse "gipt," meaning "something given." German "Gift" comes from a different etymological root—originally "geben" (to give)—but evolved to mean specifically poison. The word represents a dark moment in language: something that was "given" became something given with lethal intent. Medieval German texts refer to poisoning as "Gift geben" (to give poison). "Das ist Gift" = "That is poison." Never use this word to mean a birthday present. The German word for that is "Geschenk" (literally "something given," from "schenken," a different verb meaning "to give as a gift"). The false friendship here is particularly troubling because the words look identical yet point to such different concepts—one about joy and giving, the other about death and harm.
Wrong: "I gave him a Gift for his birthday." ✗ (implies you poisoned him!)
Right: "I gave him a Geschenk for his birthday." ✓
Memory trick: German "Gift" = poison. English "gift" = present. Never confuse them. In German, you give someone a "Geschenk," not a "Gift."

The third false friend seems to describe character, but it describes something far less impressive.

Brav sounds like "brave" and looks almost identical, but a "braves Kind" is not a courageous child. This word carries entirely different connotations.

brav
well-behaved, obedient, docile, good (especially children), compliant, proper
Etymology: Middle High German, from Old French "brave" (bold, fierce). Both English and German inherited the same word but diverged radically in meaning. English kept the courageous sense; German evolved toward the obedient sense.
English "brave" suggests courage, fearlessness, strength in the face of danger. German "brav" is almost the opposite—it means obedient, well-behaved, docile, compliant with rules. A "braves Kind" is not a courageous child, but a good, obedient one who sits quietly and doesn't cause trouble. A "braves Verhalten" is proper, conformist behavior. The word has a faintly old-fashioned, condescending tone—it's something you might say about a dog who sits when told, or a child who minds their manners. The divergence between English and German is complete and absolute. German has "mutig" for brave in the English sense (from "Mut," courage). The false friendship here is particularly insidious because both languages borrowed the same word from French but evolved it in completely opposite directions over centuries.
Wrong: "The brave child ran into the fire." (suggests foolish obedience)
Right: "The mutige child ran into the fire." (suggests courageous action)
Memory trick: "Brav" = obedient, like a well-trained dog. "Brave" = courageous, like a warrior. Completely opposite meanings from the same word!

The fourth false friend speaks of wisdom, though it refers to something more practical.

Rat is not an animal with whiskers and a tail. Nor is "Ratte" (the animal) what you need when you ask for "einen Rat."

Rat
advice, counsel, suggestion, recommendation, guidance
Etymology: Old High German "rat," from Proto-Germanic "rēd," related to "read" in the archaic English sense (to counsel, to advise). The root is connected to wisdom and counsel across Germanic languages.
The German "Rat" comes from an old Germanic root meaning "counsel" or "to advise." English "rat" took a completely different path, referring to the animal. "Ich brauche einen Rat" = "I need some advice" or "I need a piece of counsel." "Gib mir einen Rat" = "Give me a piece of advice." The false friend confusion is immediate upon hearing the word, until context shows whether we're talking about furry rodents that live in walls or helpful guidance. The animal itself in German is called a "Ratte" (plural "Ratten"), which comes from the same root as English "rat." Interestingly, "Rat" appears in compound words with its original meaning: "Rathaus" (town hall, literally "council house"), "Ratschlag" (a piece of advice, literally "council strike").
Wrong: "Ich habe einen Rat" = "I have a rat" (you have an animal!)
Right: "Ich habe einen Rat" = "I have advice" (context determines meaning)
Memory trick: German "Rat" = advice. German "Ratte" = animal. English "rat" = animal. Keep them separate.

The fifth false friend is about time, but not about the passage of time.

Eventuell looks like "eventually," but timing works differently here. The similarity is a trap that catches many English speakers.

eventuell
possibly, perhaps, maybe, potentially, conceivably, if necessary
Etymology: From Latin "eventualis," meaning "that may happen" or "contingent." Both English and German have the same Latin root, but "eventually" evolved to mean "in the end, finally, at last" (temporal), while "eventuell" kept the "possibility" meaning (conditional/probabilistic).
English "eventually" means "in the end, finally, at last"—a word strictly about time and sequence. German "eventuell" means "possibly, perhaps, maybe"—a word about probability or possibility. "Eventuelle Probleme" = "possible problems" or "potential problems." "Ich komme eventuell später" = "I might possibly come later" or "I'll maybe come later." The similarity is a trap of the cruellest kind, because both words are used frequently, and the error stands out painfully to a native speaker. A German speaker saying "Das ist eventuell möglich" is not saying "That will eventually be possible," but "That might possibly be possible." The false friendship here is structural: both languages borrowed the Latin root, but English evolved it temporally (time), while German kept it conditional (possibility).
Wrong: "Eventually I'll come to the party." → "Eventuell komme ich zur Party." (sounds uncertain)
Right: "I'll come to the party in the end." → "Am Ende komme ich zur Party." (sounds definite)
Memory trick: "Eventuell" = maybe/possibly. "Eventually" = finally/in the end. One is about probability, the other about time sequence.

The sixth false friend is about the present moment, though not about the present you receive.

Aktuell does not mean "actually" in the sense of "in reality" or "in fact." The similarity is deceptive and frequently misused.

aktuell
current, present, topical, up-to-date, of the moment, contemporary
Etymology: From Latin "actualis," meaning "relating to action" or "pertaining to the present moment." English "actually" evolved to mean "really" or "in fact" (epistemic), while German "aktuell" kept the "present/current" meaning (temporal).
Aktuell is from Latin "actualis"—relating to action or the present moment. In German, it means "current" or "present"—relating to what is happening now, not to factual reality. "Das aktuelle Problem" = "the current problem" or "the problem at hand." "Die aktuelle Situation" = "the situation as it stands now." "Aktuelle Nachrichten" = "current news" or "today's news." English "actually" evolved to mean "really" or "in fact," quite different from the temporal sense. A German speaker saying "Das ist aktuell ein Problem" is not saying "Actually, that's a problem" but "Currently, that's a problem" or "At present, that's a problem." The false friendship is particularly insidious because both words sound formal and both come from the same Latin root, but they point in completely different directions.
Wrong: "That's aktuell the problem." (sounds like you're confirming reality)
Right: "That's currently the problem." (emphasizes timing, not reality)
Memory trick: "Aktuell" = right now, current. "Actually" = really, in fact. One is about timing, the other about truth.

The seventh false friend relates to sensation and feeling, but not in the way you might expect.

Sensibel is not about being emotionally sensitive in the English way. The word describes a different kind of responsiveness.

sensibel
sensitive, responsive, touchy, easily affected, reactive, impressionable
Etymology: From Latin "sensibilis," meaning "capable of sensing" or "capable of feeling." Both languages share the same Latin root but diverged in application.
German "sensibel" comes from Latin "sensibilis"—capable of sensing or feeling. It means sensitive in a physical sense, or easily affected. "Ein sensibles Thema" = "a sensitive topic" (easily offended people, touchy subject). "Eine sensible Person" = a person easily affected or reactive. But it doesn't carry the same emotional depth or empathetic quality as English "sensitive," which often means emotionally attuned, understanding, or empathetic. A "sensible person" in English is practical and wise; a "sensible Person" in German is... well, that's not a German word construction. German has "feinfühlig" or "einfühlsam" for emotionally aware and empathetic. "Sensibel" is more about physical or emotional reactivity—being easily upset or affected—rather than being emotionally intelligent.
Wrong: "She's a sensible person." (in English = practical; in German = overly reactive)
Right: "She's a feinfühlige Person." (German = emotionally attuned)
Memory trick: "Sensibel" = easily affected, touchy. "Sensitive" in English often means empathetic. Different connotations of the same root.

The eighth and final false friend in this set concerns sympathy and likability—but they are not the same thing.

Sympathisch is not about having sympathy for someone. The word describes something more superficial but more commonly needed in daily life.

sympathisch
likeable, pleasant, appealing, nice, charming, agreeable, attractive (in personality)
Etymology: From Greek "sympatheia," meaning "sharing of feelings." English "sympathetic" evolved to mean "understanding someone's feelings or suffering." German "sympathisch" retained a sense of "liking" or "finding pleasant."
English "sympathetic" means understanding someone's feelings, or feeling for them—emotional identification and empathy. German "sympathisch" means likeable or pleasant—someone you find nice or attractive as a person. "Ich finde ihn sympathisch" = "I find him likeable" or "He's a nice person" or "He appeals to me." There's no emotional identification required, no deep understanding of suffering or feeling. You can find someone "sympathisch" without sympathizing with them at all. You might like someone's company without caring about their problems. The words look identical but point in very different directions. German uses "mitfühlend" or "verständnisvoll" for sympathetic in the English sense.
Wrong: "I am sympathisch toward his situation." (implies you like him as a person)
Right: "I am sympathetic toward his situation." (implies you understand his pain)
Memory trick: "Sympathisch" = likeable person. "Sympathetic" = understanding pain. One is about personality, the other about empathy.

Understanding False Friends: False friends are not errors to shame you. They are treasures showing how languages diverge. Two cousins, born from the same parent, grow up in different lands and become different people. They may have the same face, but they think differently, speak differently, want different things. Some words narrowed in meaning (English "chef" to cooking only), while others broadened (German "Chef" remains any boss). Some words reversed meaning entirely (English "brave" vs. German "brav"). To master a language, you must learn not just similarity, but the specific places where similarity ends. The false friends teach you humility in translation.

Lesen & Hören

Mein Freund Thomas bekommt seinen ersten Job in Deutschland, aber er ist nervös wegen der falschen Freunde in der Sprache.
Seine Mutter gibt ihm ein Wörterbuch und sagt: "Gift bedeutet nicht Geschenk—Gift ist Gift!"
Thomas lernt schnell: Ein "braves Kind" ist nicht mutig, sondern artig und gehorsam.
Sein Chef sagt: "Die aktuellen Zahlen sind sehr gut." Das Wort "aktuell" bedeutet JETZT, nicht irgendwann später.
Eine Kollegin ist "sehr sensibel"—das bedeutet leicht zu verletzen, nicht weise und praktisch.
Jetzt versteht Thomas: Diese falschen Freunde sind Fallen für Englischsprachige, aber er merkt sie sich!
